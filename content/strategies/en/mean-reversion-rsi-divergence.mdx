## How It Works

The Mean Reversion RSI Divergence strategy exploits a well-documented market phenomenon: when price makes a new low but the RSI (Relative Strength Index) makes a higher low, it signals that selling pressure is weakening even as price drops. This **bullish divergence** often precedes a reversal — a mean reversion back toward fair value.

### The Core Signal: RSI Divergence

RSI measures the magnitude of recent price changes on a scale of 0-100. In a healthy downtrend, both price and RSI should be falling together. When they diverge — price falling but RSI rising — it suggests that the selling momentum is exhausting:

1. **Price makes a lower low**: The market appears to be continuing its downtrend
2. **RSI makes a higher low**: But the momentum behind the selling is actually weakening
3. **Divergence confirmed**: This disconnect between price and momentum creates the setup

The opposite applies for bearish divergence at resistance levels (price makes higher high, RSI makes lower high).

### Multi-Timeframe Confirmation

A divergence on a single timeframe can be a false signal. This strategy requires confirmation across multiple timeframes to filter out noise:

- **Primary timeframe (5-min)**: The divergence must be visible here for entry timing
- **Confirmation timeframe (1-hour)**: The broader trend context must support a reversal (e.g., RSI not deeply oversold on the higher timeframe while signaling oversold on the lower)
- **Trend timeframe (4-hour)**: Used to determine whether the current move is a pullback within an uptrend (higher probability) or a continuation in a downtrend (lower probability)

The strategy assigns higher confidence to divergences that occur as pullbacks within higher-timeframe uptrends, as these have a significantly higher success rate.

### Volume Profile Confirmation

Beyond RSI divergence, the strategy checks the volume profile around the divergence zone:

- **Declining volume on the second low**: Confirms that sellers are losing interest
- **Volume node proximity**: The divergence occurring near a high-volume node (a price level with historically high trading activity) increases the probability of a bounce

### Entry and Exit

**Entry**: When all conditions align — RSI divergence on the primary timeframe, multi-timeframe confirmation, and supportive volume profile — the strategy enters a position at the current price.

**Exit**: Mean reversion trades have a natural target — the "mean" itself. The strategy targets:
- **Primary target**: The 20-period moving average on the primary timeframe
- **Extended target**: The upper Bollinger Band if momentum is strong
- **Stop loss**: Below the divergence low, typically 1-2 ATR below the lowest point

### Why Mean Reversion Works in Crypto

Crypto markets are often portrayed as purely momentum-driven, but mean reversion is equally prevalent — especially within established ranges and during consolidation phases. After sharp sell-offs, crypto assets frequently bounce as:

1. Leveraged short positions get liquidated (short squeeze)
2. Algorithmic buyers step in at support levels
3. Funding rates normalize, removing the cost of holding long positions

The key is identifying *which* dips are worth buying. RSI divergence with multi-timeframe confirmation is one of the most reliable filters for this purpose.

## Known Limitations

- **Trend continuation risk**: In strong downtrends, divergences can persist for extended periods before any reversal occurs. The strategy may experience several small losses before catching the actual bottom
- **Latency**: Multi-timeframe confirmation adds latency to entries, which means the strategy sometimes enters after the optimal price
- **Choppy consolidation**: In tight ranges where price oscillates without clear divergence patterns, the strategy may overtrade
